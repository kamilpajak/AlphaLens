## Anti-pattern flags

Below are papers / themes that look tempting for alpha but run straight into the failure modes you already experienced or fail one or more of your five filters. These are *not* recommendations, but “what to avoid getting excited about” on the next scan.

### 1. Retail microstructure & order flow

You asked for: **retail order flow + patient limit orders** (microstructure for retail) and potential edges that are (i) retail-replicable, (ii) not your already-closed paradigms.

**What shows up in 2024–2026 OA space:**

- A lot of market-structure commentary about **AI in trading**, tokenization, and liquidity, but **not** rigorous, 15y+ panel microstructure with net-of-cost tests.  
  - Example: Coalition Greenwich’s 2026 “Top market structure trends to watch in 2026” discusses AI being applied to research more than trading, equity-market-structure “renaissance,” and tokenized collateral, but it’s descriptive, not a tradable-stat paper.[8]

**Why they fail triage:**

- **Filter 1 (≥ 15y sample & post‑2010 OOS)**  
  Most recent microstructure papers looking at retail behavior or new venues cover **post‑2014–2015** structural changes only and therefore fall short of 15 years. You routinely see 2016–2023 windows.
- **Filter 2 (net of realistic costs)**  
  Retail microstructure work is often execution- or venue-centric; it rarely reports *portfolio* net returns including *retail-level* fees and realistic slippage. Many are about price impact or price discovery, not tradeable alpha.
- **Filter 3 (public data)**  
  Nearly all strong retail-flow work uses **proprietary order-book / PFOF / wholesaler data** (Citadel, Virtu, or exchange feed with non-retail pricing tiers), violating your public-data requirement.
- **Filter 5 (multiple-testing correction)**  
  Microstructure is still largely single- or few-hypothesis testing. Formal FDR/BH corrections are rare.

**Anti-pattern flag:**  
Many recent “retail flow alpha” angles collapse into **short-horizon mean-reversion or intraday momentum** that:
- Live in small/micro-cap symbols with poor depth,  
- Require extremely low commissions and sub‑penny routing advantages, or  
- Are effectively **HFT / market-making** strategies.[5]

For a retail quant, they **resemble your “small-cap momentum” and “8-K event-driven” failures**: they look great in-sample with top-decile “illiquid” names, but net-of-cost, post‑2020 OOS decay plus adverse selection from wholesalers kills them.

**Background papers worth knowing (Verdict: SKIP — background reading)**  
These are pre‑2024 and do not satisfy your 15y+ plus post‑2010 OOS requirement, but they are canonical for thinking about the basket:

- **Barber, Odean “The Behavior of Individual Investors” (review).**  
  Synthesizes evidence that retail order flow is predominantly *contrarian and loss-making* once costs are considered; much of the “edge” is on the **other side** of retail, not for retail.  
  → Useful as a conceptual baseline that **any new claimed retail edge must beat**.

- **Easley, Kiefer, O’Hara, Paperman – classic PIN / information-based microstructure.**  
  Shows how informed vs. uninformed trading shapes spread and price impact; instructive for why retail “patient limit orders” often earn the spread at the cost of adverse selection.

- **Hendershott & Riordan-type work on algorithmic trading and liquidity.**  
  Demonstrates that **automation tends to arbitrage away simple order-book signals**—relevant to explaining why retail-accessible limit-order alpha is crowded quickly.

All three would be **Verdict: SKIP (background reading)**: they don’t provide a viable, new 2025+ edge but guard you against being seduced by shallow “Robinhood order-flow edge” stories.

### 2. LLM analysis of 10‑K intangibles / risk disclosures

You asked for: **LLM-driven analysis of 10‑K intangibles / risk disclosures** (explicitly not 8‑K), with robust post‑2020 OOS and public data.

**What exists 2024–2026 (OA):**

- A cluster of ML / LLM finance papers that:
  - Use **10‑K text embeddings, fine-tuned transformers, or proprietary LLMs** to build sentiment / risk / intangible-capital measures.
  - Evaluate on **2010–2023** 10‑K corpora, often with narrow ~12–13 year windows.
  - Test long–short portfolios sorted on LLM-based scores.
- Several cross-asset or factor papers embed LLM text features as additional signals, but usually in **institutional-style multi-signal models** rather than standalone retail-tradable factors.

**Systematic triage failures:**

1. **Sample period & OOS (Filter 1)**  
   - Most 10‑K+LLM papers begin around **2004–2006** (EDGAR XML era), but their *effective* sample tends to be 2006–2022.  
   - Only a minority do **explicit multi-window OOS**, and those who do rarely isolate *post‑2020* as a distinct regime with proper holdout.

2. **Net returns & turnover (Filter 2)**  
   - The majority report **gross long–short factor returns**, not net after realistic trading costs, and allow **high turnover**, especially when rebalancing quarterly or more often on textual features.  
   - Some only show **information ratios** without cost modeling.

3. **Data replicability (Filter 3)**  
   - Many rely on **commercial text databases (e.g., Capital IQ, Refinitiv, RavenPack)** or **closed LLM APIs**; the pure EDGAR-based approaches are closer to your standard but often incorporate proprietary labeling or vendor-provided mappings.  
   - When they do use public EDGAR, the **LLM itself is often proprietary** (paid API fine-tuned for the paper).

4. **Multiple testing (Filter 5)**  
   - Text-based alpha papers typically try several model architectures, features, or score transforms and **do not run BH / Bonferroni / White Reality Check** over the whole model space; they present one “winner” specification.

**Anti-pattern flag:**  
The majority of these papers, especially those branded as “LLM research assistant” or “Buffett-style LLM stock picker,” **echo your Layer 2g failure**:
- They tilt toward **quality / value / low-vol / profitability** baskets,  
- Outperform mainly in **pre‑2018** data, and  
- Show **severe performance decay in the 2020s growth regime**, sometimes explicitly documenting value underperformance.[14]

T. Rowe Price’s note “When valuation fails” (2025) is a practitioner-level example: they detail cases where classical valuation disciplines lag systematically.[14] That piece is not an LLM paper, but it underscores your observation that value-style lenses (including LLMs that learn “Buffett-like” narratives) structurally struggled in the 2020s.

**Background papers worth knowing (Verdict: SKIP — background reading)**

- **Loughran & McDonald-style dictionaries for 10‑K risk and tone.**  
  Foundation for mapping 10‑K text to risk sentiment and litigation/hardship language.

- **Early transformer-based 10‑K embedding papers (2019–2021 q‑fin / arXiv).**  
  They show some incremental predictive power over bag-of-words scores but suffer from:
  - Limited sample (a decade or so),
  - Gross returns only,
  - No multiple-testing correction,
  - And strong overlap with **value/quality** exposures, making them conceptually close to your Layer 2g.

All would be **Verdict: SKIP (background reading)**.

### 3. Cross-asset overlays (commodity / FX overlays on equity factors)

You asked for cross-asset overlays: **commodity / FX overlays on equity factors**, ideally showing persistent, retail-executable alpha with post‑2020 OOS.

**What’s visible 2024–2026 (OA & practitioner):**

- Institutional commentary (e.g., JPMorgan podcasts / notes) on **alpha in commodities trading** and overlay strategies that use **FX, rates, and vol** to enhance equity portfolios.[11]
- Practitioner articles on “alpha‑enhanced equity” with quant overlays combining factors, macro and option-based adjustments.[15]

**How they fail your filters:**

1. **Sample & OOS (Filter 1)**  
   - Practitioner pieces (like JPM’s “Guide to finding alpha in commodities trading”[11] and Goldman’s alpha-enhanced equity note[15]) are more conceptual than backtest papers; when they present performance, the horizon is often <15 years and without a clean pre‑/post‑2010 split.
2. **Net returns & turnover (Filter 2)**  
   - They do not present fully specified **net performance with retail-executable assumptions**. Commodity overlays frequently assume institutional futures commissions, margining, and financing costs.
3. **Public data (Filter 3)**  
   - Many overlay strategies rely on **internal signals, proprietary risk models**, or trade data.
4. **n / multi-asset & multiple testing (Filters 4–5)**  
   - Strategy-level presentations lack the structure of an academic factor paper and almost never implement explicit multiple-testing corrections.

**Anti-pattern flag:**  
Many overlays reduce to:
- **Timing factors using macro indicators** (e.g., rate/FX regimes), or  
- Levering factor exposures via **commodity or FX carry**, often with substantial leverage and path dependence.

These overlap with your **tactical sector rotation** failure:
- High model degrees of freedom,  
- Easy to explain in narratives but hard to validate statistically,  
- Very sensitive to window choice and regime definitions.

**Background papers worth knowing (Verdict: SKIP — background reading)**

- Core **cross-asset risk-parity / global macro factor** literature (pre‑2019):  
  - Equity–bond–commodity–FX carry and value overlays that demonstrate long historical Sharpe but often with:
    - Institutional execution assumptions,
    - No explicit multiple-testing correction,
    - Limited or weak post‑2010–2012 OOS validation.

These remain conceptually helpful but should be **SKIP (background reading)** under your standards.

### 4. Factor decay 2025+ with OOS validation post‑2020

You asked for: **factor decay 2025+ with OOS validation post‑2020**, and ideally some sign of a **regime shift that might invalidate the “value structurally underperforms” thesis** or otherwise motivate new alpha.

**What 2024–2026 OA / practitioner pieces show:**

- General quant commentaries (e.g., Goldman’s “How quant strategies drive the alpha enhanced approach to equity investing” in 2025[15]) acknowledge:
  - **Crowding and factor decay** in standard value, size, momentum, low-vol,
  - Need for **adaptive, multi-signal models**,
  - But they do not provide a 15y+ academically rigorous factor-decay study with multiple-testing correction.
- Some technical pieces (including arXiv q‑fin like new optimisation frameworks[9] or multi-agent systems for factor discovery[13]) demonstrate:
  - ML-based factor construction,
  - Portfolio optimisation improvements,
  - But not the clean kind of single-factor OOS decay analysis you are asking for.

**Why they fail the triage:**

1. **Filter 1 (15y sample & post‑2010 OOS)**  
   - Many ML/factor papers have **short samples** (e.g., 2010–2023) or treat 2018–2022 as the only OOS, not a second or third distinct regime.
2. **Filter 2 (net, low turnover)**  
   - Almost all ML-driven factor constructions (e.g., transformer-based, momentum-gated optimisers[9], cross-asset “alpha frameworks”[13]) have **high turnover** and show **gross** rather than net returns.
3. **Filter 5 (multiple testing)**  
   - Complex ML architectures try many model variants; formal FDR/BH or White Reality Check over a rich hyperparameter grid is essentially absent.

**Anti-pattern flag 1 — “new momentum” that is old momentum**

- Work like **“A Momentum-Gated Hierarchical Optimisation Framework”**[9] improves risk allocation:
  - Conceptually interesting for portfolio construction,
  - But the edge largely comes from **standard momentum signals** combined with smarter downside-risk treatment.
- This directly collides with your **Layer 2b: small-cap themed momentum** failure:
  - Core signal is still cross-sectional momentum,
  - Shorter samples and complex optimisation make it easy to overfit,
  - No multiple-testing control across model variants.

**Anti-pattern flag 2 — “AI factor discovery frameworks”**

- Papers like **“AlphaCrafter: A Full-Stack Multi-Agent Framework for Cross-Asset Alpha Factor Discovery”**[13]:
  - Use multiple agents and LLMs to generate and test a large space of candidate factors.
  - Are exactly what your triage is designed to guard against:
    - Huge multiple-testing problem,
    - Limited explicit corrections,
    - Often short, post‑2010 samples,
    - Heavy reliance on proprietary or non-standard data.

These frameworks can be useful meta-tools for *your* research, but **none should be treated as evidence of a robust, deployable factor** under your 5-filter rule.

**Anti-pattern flag 3 — “value is back” stories**

- Practitioner pieces that claim “valuation is working again” in brief windows (e.g., during 2022–2023 inflation spikes) often:
  - Cherry-pick the start date right after value’s trough,
  - Ignore drawdowns in 2020–2021 and early 2024,
  - Do not provide 15y+ histories with post‑2010 OOS splits and multiple-test corrections.

Given your **Layer 2g** experience and the T. Rowe Price piece explicitly titled **“When valuation fails”**[14], any short-window “value comeback” narrative should be treated as **anecdotal** rather than evidence for Trigger 3.
